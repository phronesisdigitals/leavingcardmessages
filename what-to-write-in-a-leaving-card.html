👋 Leaving cards

What to Write in a Leaving Card: A Complete Guide

Every week, thousands of people across UK offices are handed a leaving card and immediately freeze. Here's exactly what to write — for every type of colleague and situation.

📖 5 min readUpdated 2025

You've been handed a leaving card. Maybe it's for someone you've worked alongside for five years. Maybe it's for someone from the third floor whose name you had to check on the company directory. Either way, you're standing there with a pen, and everyone else seems to have already written something.

This guide covers every scenario — so you can write something genuine, appropriate, and quick.

Why leaving cards are so hard to write

The blank card problem is real, and it's not because people are lazy or uncaring. It's because leaving cards sit in an awkward middle ground: they need to feel personal without being over the top, warm without being cloying, and professional without being stiff.

The result? Most people fall back on "good luck in your new adventure!" — which lands with all the emotional weight of a biscuit tin label.

The golden rule: One specific detail — a memory, a trait, an inside reference — makes any message feel personal. You don't need a long message. You need a real one.

What to write for a colleague you know well

This is the easiest scenario because you have material to work with. Think about: a specific memory, something they always said or did, how they made the team better, or what you'll genuinely miss.

Example

"Working with you has been one of the highlights of my time here. I'll miss your ability to make any Monday morning feel slightly more survivable. Wishing you everything you deserve in the next chapter — which is a lot."

Example — shorter

"Five years of putting up with each other — and I'd do it all again. Go smash it. You'll be brilliant."

What to write for a colleague you barely know

This is the one that causes genuine panic. You don't have a shared memory to draw on. The trick is to be warm and genuine without pretending a closeness that doesn't exist.

Example

"We may not have worked together directly, but you've always been a familiar and friendly face around the office. Wishing you all the best in whatever comes next."

Example — even simpler

"Best of luck with the next chapter — hoping it's everything you're hoping for."

What to write for your manager or boss

Tone is everything here. You want to be genuine and warm, but avoid anything that sounds sycophantic. Focus on what you actually learned from them or how they shaped your time there.

Example

"You've been a brilliant manager — the kind that actually makes people want to do their best work. Thank you for everything you've done for the team. Wherever you're headed, they're lucky to have you."

What to write for a junior colleague or someone you've mentored

This is a chance to be encouraging and express genuine pride without it sounding like a school report.

Example

"Watching you grow in this role has been a genuine pleasure. You came in ready to learn and you're leaving having taught a few of us a thing or two as well. Go and do great things — we'll be watching."

What to write based on why they're leaving

New job

Keep it forward-looking and positive. Wish them well without implying their new place is better or worse than where they're leaving.

Example

"Exciting times ahead — they're getting someone brilliant and they might not even know it yet. Wishing you every success."

Retirement

Acknowledge the milestone properly. Years of service deserve more than a generic message.

Example

"After everything you've given to this place over the years, you've more than earned what comes next. Enjoy every single moment of it."

Travelling or gap year

Keep it fun and energetic — this is a happy occasion.

Example

"Going off to see the world while we're stuck here in the Tuesday 9am meeting — genuinely jealous. Have the most brilliant time."

Quick tips for any leaving card message

  • Keep it to 2–3 sentences — card space is limited and shorter is often more powerful
  • Use their name if you can — it immediately makes a message feel more personal
  • Avoid clichés like "best of luck on your new adventure" — they read as filler
  • If you're funny in real life, be funny — just keep it warm rather than sharp
  • End with something forward-looking — a wish, a hope, or an expression of confidence in them
  • Proofread before you write it in the card — you can't use Ctrl+Z on a pen
