What to Write in a Sympathy Card: Gentle Guidance

Sympathy cards are the hardest cards to write. The fear of saying the wrong thing often leads to saying nothing meaningful at all. This guide helps you find the right words.

📖 5 min readUpdated 2025

When someone we know is grieving or going through real pain, the instinct is to say something — anything — to help. But the blank card feels impossible. What if you say the wrong thing? What if it makes it worse?

The truth is: the act of writing at all matters more than the words. A genuine, simple message will always mean more than silence. This guide helps you get there.

The most important thing to understand

You are not trying to fix anything. Sympathy messages aren't there to make the pain go away — they can't. They're there to say: I see you, I'm thinking of you, you're not alone. That's enough. That's everything.

Once you let go of the idea that your words need to solve something, writing becomes much easier.

What to write when someone has lost a loved one

Keep it simple, warm, and genuine. Don't try to explain grief or offer perspective — just acknowledge the loss and express care.

Example — colleague or acquaintance

"I'm so sorry for your loss. Please know that you're in my thoughts during this time. I hope you're surrounded by people who love you."

Example — close friend

"There are no words that feel right at a time like this, so I'll just say: I love you, I'm here, and I'm not going anywhere. Take all the time you need."

Example — if you knew the person who passed

"I'm so sorry. [Name] was such a wonderful person and it was a privilege to know them. Thinking of you and your family with so much love."

What to write for a serious illness

Here you want to express support without making assumptions about the outcome. Focus on being present rather than predicting recovery.

Example

"I'm thinking of you so much right now. Please don't worry about anything except looking after yourself — everything else can wait. I'm here if you need anything at all."

Example — shorter

"Sending you so much strength and warmth. You don't have to be brave all the time. We're all here for you."

What to write for a miscarriage or pregnancy loss

This requires particular care. Acknowledge the loss directly — don't dance around it. Many people find vague messages more painful than honest ones.

Example

"I'm so deeply sorry for your loss. This is a real grief and it deserves to be felt. I'm thinking of you and I'm here — there's no rush to be okay."

What to write for a general difficult time

Example

"I don't know exactly what you're going through, but I want you to know that you're not going through it alone. Thinking of you."

Things to avoid in a sympathy card

  • "Everything happens for a reason" — this is rarely comforting and often painful
  • "I know how you feel" — you don't, and grief is deeply personal
  • "They're in a better place now" — only appropriate if you know this is shared belief
  • "At least..." — any sentence starting with this minimises the loss
  • "Stay strong" — it puts pressure on someone who may need permission to fall apart
  • Long messages that make the card about your discomfort with the situation
